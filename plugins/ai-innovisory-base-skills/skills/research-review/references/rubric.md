# Research review rubric — detailed scoring guidance

The rubric has nine criteria. Each is scored Pass / Partial / Fail. The pass rule applies on top: a Fail on Source quality, Traceability, or Argument quality sends the overall verdict to Draft only.

This file is the reference for how to score each criterion. Read it when you're unsure where a piece sits between bands. Most calibration questions are answered here. Criteria 1–7 are shared with the fact-check skill; criteria 8 and 9 are specific to research review.

## 1. Source quality

**Review question:** Are the sources credible, current and relevant?

**Pass.** The piece relies on primary, official, or reputable secondary sources for its load-bearing claims. "Load-bearing" means: claims the conclusion depends on. Examples of acceptable sources:
- Primary: the company's own annual report, a regulator's published guidance, the original peer-reviewed paper, the named dataset, the actual filing.
- Official: government statistical office, standards body, central bank, named organisation's own statements.
- Reputable secondary: established quality press, recognised research institutions, trade press with editorial standards.

**Partial.** A mix — some primary or reputable, but with notable reliance on weaker sources for important claims. Or sources are reputable but ageing (more than 3–5 years old for fast-moving topics) without acknowledgement.

**Fail.** Load-bearing claims are sourced to anonymous blog posts, content farms, marketing material, AI-generated summaries of other sources, or unnamed sources. Or no sources at all are given.

Note: a single weak source is not automatic Fail. The question is whether the *conclusion* depends on weak sourcing.

## 2. Evidence separation

**Review question:** Does it separate evidence from interpretation?

**Pass.** Facts, assumptions, and judgements are visibly distinguished — through phrasing ("the data show", "this suggests", "in our view"), through structure (an "evidence" section separate from an "implications" section), or through explicit labelling. A reader can tell which sentences are reporting and which are interpreting.

**Partial.** The separation is mostly there but slips in places — interpretation phrased as fact, or evidence and judgement blended without flagging. Recoverable on a careful read.

**Fail.** Interpretation is presented as fact throughout. The reader cannot easily tell what was observed versus what was inferred. Phrases like "clearly", "obviously", "the truth is" used to do interpretive work without supporting evidence.

## 3. Currency

**Review question:** Are dates and recency handled properly?

**Pass.** Publication dates are named for sources where currency matters. Where the piece relies on data with a known cadence (annual reports, quarterly figures, regulatory updates), it uses the most recent reasonably available. Stale data is acknowledged as such.

**Partial.** Dates are inconsistent — some sources dated, others not. The piece uses some recent material and some clearly outdated material without distinction.

**Fail.** Sources are undated, or the piece relies on stale data without acknowledgement for a topic where recency matters. Quoting a 2020 figure as if it were current in 2026, for example, with no note that a more recent figure exists.

Currency is topic-sensitive. A historical analysis can legitimately use 50-year-old sources. A market scan cannot.

## 4. Coverage

**Review question:** Does it include the main perspectives or variables?

**Pass.** The piece addresses the main angles on its question. For a market scan, that means more than one competitor. For a policy brief, that means the case for and against. For a competitor summary, that means strengths and weaknesses. The piece does not omit a perspective that an informed reader would expect to see addressed.

**Partial.** Most of the main perspectives are present, but one or two are missing or treated superficially. The piece is not actively misleading by omission, but it is incomplete.

**Fail.** The piece cherry-picks evidence that supports a single conclusion and omits or minimises evidence that would complicate it. Or it addresses only one perspective on a question where multiple are obviously relevant.

Coverage is the criterion where AI-generated research most often falls short — it tends to produce a coherent single view rather than surface the trade-offs.

## 5. Traceability

**Review question:** Can a reviewer verify the claims?

**Pass.** Important claims can be traced back to a specific source — through inline citation, footnote, named attribution ("according to the FT…"), or a clearly-linked bibliography. A reviewer could check the original within a reasonable effort.

**Partial.** Some important claims are traceable, others are not. Or sourcing is general ("research shows…") without naming the research.

**Fail.** Important claims are unsourced. Statements of fact have no attribution. The reader has to take the piece's word for everything. A bibliography at the end without inline links to specific claims does not pass — the reader cannot tell which source supports which claim.

This is one of three blocking criteria. A Fail here sends the overall verdict to Draft only.

## 6. Contradictions

**Review question:** Does it identify where sources disagree?

**Pass.** Where the evidence is genuinely contested — sources disagree, data is uncertain, the consensus is shifting — the piece surfaces this. Phrases like "estimates range from X to Y", "the picture is unclear", "two views are in play". It does not pretend to certainty it doesn't have.

**Partial.** Some contradictions are surfaced, others are smoothed over. The piece acknowledges uncertainty in places but presents contested claims as settled in others.

**Fail.** The piece presents a clean, certain narrative on questions where the underlying evidence is contested. No acknowledgement that sources disagree, no flagging of uncertainty. This is the canonical AI-research failure mode — synthesising into a confident-sounding answer when the underlying material doesn't support that confidence.

## 7. Decision value

**Review question:** Does it help someone decide what to do?

**Pass.** The piece ends (or includes a clearly-marked section) with implications, trade-offs, or open questions. The reader knows what to do with the information, or knows what to ask next. It does not stop at "here is the information".

**Partial.** Implications are implicit — the reader has to do the inferential work themselves. The piece is informative but not yet decision-ready.

**Fail.** Pure description with no "so what". The reader is left with a body of facts and no guidance on how to act, or even on what the open questions are.

Decision value checks for *presence* — does the piece attempt to help the reader decide at all. It is distinct from Insight depth (criterion 9), which checks whether that attempt is any good.

## 8. Argument quality

**Review question:** Does the reasoning actually support the conclusion?

**Pass.** Every material step in the argument chain is either backed by evidence in the piece or clearly flagged as an assumption. Causal claims rule out obvious alternative explanations, or at least acknowledge them. Conclusions are proportionate to the evidence — a piece that found a modest correlation doesn't conclude a strong causal relationship. No unstated leaps between evidence and recommendation.

**Partial.** The argument mostly holds but has one soft spot — a causal claim stated more strongly than the evidence supports, a step in the chain that's plausible but unstated, or a conclusion that slightly outruns what was actually shown. A careful reader would ask one clarifying question and be satisfied.

**Fail.** The conclusion does not follow from the stated evidence. Common patterns:
- **Non-sequitur** — the recommendation doesn't connect to the evidence at all, or connects only by association.
- **Unsupported leap** — a critical step in the reasoning is simply asserted, with no evidence and no acknowledgement that it's an assumption.
- **False dichotomy** — the piece frames two options as exhaustive when others clearly exist, then reasons from that narrowed frame.
- **Correlation as causation** — two trends move together and the piece asserts causation without ruling out confounders, reverse causation, or coincidence.
- **Motivated framing** — the evidence appears to have been selected and sequenced to arrive at a predetermined conclusion, rather than the conclusion following from an honest reading of the evidence. Look for this especially when the framing matches what the brief or the user's stated view would want to hear.

This is the second criterion unique to research review, and one of the three blocking criteria — a piece whose argument doesn't hold cannot be decision-ready no matter how well-sourced its component facts are. Fail here even if every individual claim is Confirmed; sourced-but-illogical is still not safe to act on.

**Scoring note:** this is the criterion reviewers most often score too generously. It is tempting to read a confident, well-written conclusion and assume the reasoning underneath is equally solid. Write out the actual argument chain (step 2 of the workflow) before judging this — do not score it from a fluent read alone.

## 9. Insight depth

**Review question:** Does it surface something a reasonably informed reader didn't already know, or just restate the obvious?

**Pass.** At least one implication, trade-off, or recommendation is specific to this research — it names a concrete threshold, a named option with a named cost, a blind spot the reader wouldn't have anticipated, or a next question that could only be asked having done this work. A test: if you could swap the conclusion into an unrelated report on a different topic and it would still read as true, it has not passed this criterion.

**Partial.** The piece has one strong, specific insight buried among mostly generic statements, or all of its implications are directionally sound but phrased so broadly ("stakeholders should monitor developments") that they don't actually commit to anything.

**Fail.** Every implication is generic enough to apply to almost any piece of research on almost any topic. Classic tells: "this presents both opportunities and challenges", "organisations should carefully consider the risks and benefits", "further monitoring is recommended". These are true of nearly everything and give the reader nothing to act on.

Insight depth is not a blocking criterion — a piece can fail it and still be factually sound and safe to use as reference material. But research that fails this criterion has no advantage over a plain summary of its sources; the analytical work hasn't actually produced anything.

**Scoring note:** distinguish this from Decision value (criterion 7). A piece can Pass Decision value (it has a clearly-marked "implications" section) and still Fail Insight depth (that section is boilerplate). The two are independent — check both.

## Calibration notes

A few patterns to watch for:

- **Confident hedging.** A piece that uses lots of qualifiers ("may", "could", "potentially") without any actual verification or sourcing is not passing Evidence separation or Traceability — it is just hiding behind language. Flag this.
- **Bibliography theatre.** A long list of sources at the end with no inline citations does not pass Traceability. The reviewer cannot match sources to claims.
- **One-source amplification.** A piece that cites multiple sources but they all trace back to a single original (e.g., three news articles all citing the same press release) is a single source. Score accordingly under Source quality.
- **Anchored framing.** Where a piece's framing comes from the brief or the user's prior view, ask whether opposing framings were even considered. If not, that's a Coverage problem — and check whether it's also an Argument quality problem (motivated framing).
- **Fluent-but-hollow.** The most common failure mode for AI-generated or AI-assisted research: well-organised, confidently written, fully readable — and, once you write out the actual argument chain, either unsupported at a key step (criterion 8) or landing on conclusions that say nothing specific (criterion 9). Fluency is not evidence of soundness. Score the chain, not the prose.
- **AI tells.** Generic implications ("organisations should consider...", "stakeholders should be aware...") are Partial at best on Decision value and a Fail on Insight depth. Real decision value and real insight name specific trade-offs and specific next moves.
